# Winthrop Street Purchase and Sale Agreement — Analysis
## 2026 ATM Article 1 — Summary, Valuation, and Risks

## Part 2: Key Transaction Terms

**Buyer's Contingencies (all must be met for the sale to close):**
- CPC recommendation and Town Meeting authorization, with appropriation of purchase price (by June 1, 2026, extendable to August 31, 2026)
- G.L. c. 30B compliance: uniqueness determination filed with the Central Register, 30-day objection period
- Premises delivered free and clear of all tenants, occupants, and leases; written waivers of relocation benefits from all occupants; seller waives own relocation rights
- Physical inspection satisfactory to buyer (in buyer's sole and absolute discretion), including subsurface environmental assessment
- Compliance with all applicable state laws for municipal real property acquisition

**The Seller Condition (tenant/occupant clearance)** has an extended deadline: if not satisfied by August 31, 2026, the closing shall be continued until satisfied, with a hard stop of **August 31, 2027**. This 16-month extension window is the most unusual feature of the agreement. It signals either the existence of current occupants or use agreements on the premises that are not immediately terminable.

**Seller's Warranties (Section 20):**
- Good and clear, record and marketable title in fee simple
- No options, rights of first refusal, or other contracts to purchase
- No leases, licenses, or occupancy agreements
- No litigation affecting the premises
- **To the best of SELLER's knowledge:** no oil, hazardous materials, or hazardous waste; no underground storage tanks; no chlordane used as pesticide; no asbestos, lead paint, or unsafe radon concentrations; no environmental notices from any governmental authority

**Rollback Tax Waiver:** The Town explicitly agrees that the Seller has no obligation to pay rollback taxes. This is addressed in both the P&S (Section 14) and reflects a meaningful dollar concession from the town (discussed below).

**No Deposit:** The agreement contains no earnest money deposit from the buyer. This is standard practice for Massachusetts municipal land acquisitions under G.L. c. 30B, as municipalities cannot typically bind themselves to financial obligations prior to appropriation.

**Broker:** Paul Harkey is the seller's agent. The seller is solely responsible for any commission. The town represents that it has not engaged a broker.

**Inspection Rights:** 48-hour oral notice sufficient; buyer may conduct any tests it deems appropriate, including subsurface environmental assessments, at buyer's own risk.

**Condition at Closing:** Seller must deliver the premises substantially as they were at signing — free of tenants/occupants — but the seller shall not cut or remove trees, construct anything, install fencing, or otherwise disturb the natural condition of the premises before closing (Section 7).

## Part 3: Valuation — Is $4,850,000 a Fair Price?

### Price Per Acre

| Basis | Acres | Price/Acre |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| P&S stated ("approximately 88 acres") | 88.0 | $55,114 |
| Warrant parcel sum (38.01 + 8.00 + 0.72 + 32.00) | 78.73 | $61,603 |

At $55,000–$62,000 per acre, the purchase price is at the lower end of the range for large parcels of primarily forested, non-developable land in the suburban Boston market. For context, the Norfolk County median asking price for listed land is approximately $191,729 per acre — however, that figure reflects small, developable residential lots, not large conservation parcels.

Large conservation parcels commonly sell at significant discounts to residential lot equivalents because:
- Wetlands, slope, and setbacks reduce the developable portion
- Buyers are typically municipalities or land trusts with limited budgets
- Conservation restrictions imposed at or before sale reduce the future value further
- There is no competitive market — towns frequently acquire these through negotiated sales

### The CPA Restriction Effect

Once purchased, the land will carry a permanent CPA deed restriction. This restriction, once placed, encumbers the title in perpetuity and materially reduces the property's market value. The town is paying $4,850,000 for land that, after recording the restriction, will have a sharply lower resale value. This is intentional and appropriate for conservation purposes, but it means the purchase price should be evaluated on the basis of unrestricted value — not the post-restriction value.

### What an Independent Appraisal Would Address

The P&S does not reference an independent appraisal. For a CPA acquisition, Massachusetts practice generally requires evidence of fair market value, either through an appraisal or through a G.L. c. 30B uniqueness determination that substitutes competitive bidding. The warrant article authorizes the Select Board to proceed "upon such terms and conditions as the Select Board shall determine to be in the best interest of the Town" — which is the standard authority language. Town Meeting voters should ask whether an independent appraisal was obtained and what it concluded.

### The Total Cost of Acquisition

The $4,850,000 purchase price understates the town's actual cost:

| Cost Component | Amount |
|---------------|--------|
| Purchase price | $4,850,000 |
| Budget buffer (closing costs, legal, remediation, structure removal) | $770,000 |
| Estimated interest on $2.75M borrowing (4.5%, 20 years) | ~$1,478,000 |
| **Total estimated cost** | **~$7,098,000** |

On a per-acre basis (using the warrant's 78.73 acres), the all-in cost including debt service is approximately **$90,150 per acre**. That is a more appropriate figure for evaluating the town's total investment.

## Part 4: Risks and Concerns

### Risk 1: Acreage Discrepancy

The P&S states "approximately 88 acres." The four parcel sizes listed in the warrant article sum to 78.73 acres — a difference of 9.27 acres (nearly 12%). This discrepancy may reflect:
- Including road frontage, rights-of-way, or easement areas in the 88-acre figure
- An error in one of the documents
- The existence of additional land not described in the warrant parcels

Before Town Meeting vote, the actual recorded acreage of each parcel should be confirmed against the deeds (Norfolk Registry, Book 8613, Pages 554–557). The purchase price should be verified as applying to the correct total acreage. If the actual acreage is closer to 78.73 acres, the price-per-acre figure rises accordingly.

### Risk 2: Quitclaim Deed Provides Limited Protection

The title will be conveyed by quitclaim deed — the most common deed type in Massachusetts, but also the weakest in terms of seller warranties. A quitclaim deed only protects the buyer against defects the grantor personally created. It provides **no protection** against:
- Encumbrances, easements, or restrictions that predate the seller's ownership
- Claims arising from prior owners' actions
- Defects in earlier links in the chain of title

For an 88-acre parcel with a deed history running back to at least 1990 (and likely earlier), there may be prior easements, access rights, or restrictions that are not apparent from the current deed but that could affect the town's intended use. Title insurance is referenced (Section 17) and should be obtained. The buyer's attorney reviews title, but Town Meeting voters should confirm that title insurance will be purchased.

### Risk 3: Landscape Business Environmental Exposure

38 Winthrop Street was the operating location of Shady Oaks Landscape & Tree. A commercial landscaping and tree service operation would typically involve:
- Storage and use of pesticides, herbicides, and fertilizers
- Petroleum fuel storage (tanks for vehicles and equipment)
- Equipment maintenance with hydraulic fluids and lubricants
- Potential chlordane historical use (though chlordane was banned in 1988, pre-dating the trust)

The Seller's environmental warranty is qualified by the phrase **"to the best of SELLER's knowledge."** This is the weakest possible standard — it does not require the seller to investigate; it only requires disclosure of what the seller already knows. If there are contaminated soils or groundwater that the seller was unaware of (or has not investigated), those conditions are not covered by the warranty.

The $770,000 buffer in the appropriation explicitly includes "environmental remediation" as an anticipated cost — which suggests the town has some awareness that remediation may be needed. Town Meeting voters should ask whether a Phase I Environmental Site Assessment has been completed, and whether a Phase II (subsurface investigation) is planned before or after closing.

### Risk 4: Rollback Taxes Are a Significant Concession

The P&S explicitly relieves the seller of any obligation to pay rollback taxes (Section 14). This is a meaningful financial concession that benefits the seller at the town's expense.

Under Massachusetts G.L. c. 61, 61A, or 61B, large forested or agricultural parcels often qualify for current-use reduced property tax assessment. When such land is sold and converted to a non-qualifying use, the seller ordinarily owes rollback taxes — the difference between the reduced assessment paid and the full rate assessment, plus 5% annual interest, typically for 5 years preceding the sale.

For 78+ acres of land that may have been taxed under reduced current-use rates for years, the rollback tax obligation could be substantial — potentially $50,000–$200,000 or more depending on the assessment history, current tax rate, and how many years of current-use classification preceded the sale.

The town is taking on no additional direct liability for the rollback taxes (it is tax-exempt); rather, it is simply waiving the right to require the seller to pay them. The effect is that the seller receives the full $4,850,000 with no deduction for rollback taxes that would otherwise be owed. This is a negotiated concession that reduces the effective net payment to the town.

Town Meeting voters should ask whether the land was enrolled in Chapter 61, 61A, or 61B, what the rollback tax exposure would have been, and why the town agreed to waive this obligation rather than crediting it against the purchase price.

### Risk 5: Extended Seller Condition Deadline Suggests Occupancy Complications

The seller's obligation to deliver the premises free of tenants and occupants has a hard deadline of **August 31, 2027** — 16 months after signing. This is highly unusual. In a typical land purchase, the seller either delivers vacant land at closing or the buyer terminates.

The extended timeline suggests one or more of the following:
- There are current tenants or occupants with leases or other agreements that cannot be terminated on short notice
- The landscape business has a wind-down period with equipment or materials still on site
- There may be occupants with potential relocation benefit claims under M.G.L. c. 79A (Uniform Relocation Assistance Act), which entitles displaced occupants to benefits

The requirement for written waivers of relocation benefits from all occupants (Section 16(c)) reinforces that there are known occupants whose relocation status must be resolved. If the Seller Condition is not met by August 31, 2026, the town is contractually obligated to keep waiting — up to an additional full year — without any remedy short of termination. During that year, the town has a signed P&S and committed CPA funds, but no property.

### Risk 6: No Independent Price Validation Mechanism

Unlike competitive procurements under G.L. c. 30B (which require solicitation of bids), a uniqueness determination allows the town to negotiate directly with a single seller without competitive pricing. While this is appropriate when a specific parcel is uniquely suited to a public purpose, it also means there is no market-tested price. The $4,850,000 figure was negotiated between the seller's real estate agent and town representatives — potentially without a formal independent appraisal.

For comparison, a property at 50 Winthrop Street in Medway (not part of this transaction but on the same street) shows recent listing history that may provide useful context. However, without a formal MAI appraisal of the subject parcels, the appropriate price range is difficult to establish independently.

### Risk 7: CPA Restriction Creates a One-Way Door

The permanent CPA deed restriction required by G.L. c. 44B §12 is intentional and consistent with the town's conservation goals — but it is also irreversible without another Town Meeting vote and, in some cases, legislative action. Once the deed restriction is recorded, the properties cannot be sold, transferred, or used for non-CPA purposes without unwinding a structure that is designed to be permanent.

This means the town must be confident of its intended use before closing. The warrant article defers the designation of specific uses to "subsequent votes of Town Meeting" — which preserves flexibility, but also means the town will be committing $4,850,000+ to land whose precise use is not yet defined. Open space, recreation, and community housing have very different infrastructure requirements and community impacts.

### Risk 8: Debt Service Is a Multi-Decade Obligation

The $2,750,000 in borrowing authorized by Article 1 adds approximately $211,000 per year in debt service at 4.5% over 20 years, totaling approximately $1.48 million in interest. This is an ongoing general fund obligation, not a one-time CPA expenditure. Unlike the CPA reserve transfer (which uses money already collected), the borrowing portion creates a recurring burden on future tax rate and budget.

Combined, the CPA draw ($2.87M) depletes a substantial portion of the CPA General Reserves — the same fund that supports the annual CPA budget (ATM 14: $645,850/year) and ongoing projects. Town Meeting voters should understand the CPA reserve balance after this acquisition and how it affects future CPA capacity.

## Part 5: What Voters Should Ask

Before approving Article 1, Town Meeting voters should seek answers to the following questions:

1. **Acreage:** What is the exact recorded acreage of each parcel? Why does the P&S say "approximately 88 acres" while the warrant states 78.73 acres? Is the purchase price based on a per-acre rate or a lump sum?

2. **Appraisal:** Was an independent MAI appraisal obtained? What did it conclude? The answer should be a dollar range, not just a statement that the price is "fair."

3. **Environmental condition:** Has a Phase I ESA been completed for 38 Winthrop Street (the former Shady Oaks Landscape & Tree site)? Are any environmental concerns known? What is the basis for the $770,000 buffer including "environmental remediation"?

4. **Rollback taxes:** Is any of this land enrolled in Chapter 61, 61A, or 61B? If so, what is the estimated rollback tax exposure that the town is waiving?

5. **Occupancy:** Who currently occupies or uses the premises? What is the nature of the tenancy or use agreement that necessitates an August 31, 2027 seller condition deadline?

6. **CPA reserves:** What will the CPA General Reserves balance be after the $2.87M transfer? How does this affect the town's ability to fund future CPA projects?

7. **Title insurance:** Will the town purchase title insurance? Who is the title insurer, and are there known exceptions from coverage?

8. **Intended use:** Does the Select Board have a preliminary plan for which parcels will be designated for open space, recreation, or housing? The warrant defers this to future votes, but voters are being asked to approve $5.62M without knowing the specific intended use.

---

## Summary Assessment

The Winthrop Street acquisition is conceptually sound — large, contiguous open space parcels abutting Choate Park and the high school represent exactly the kind of strategic conservation land that CPA funds are designed to purchase. The price per acre ($55,000–$62,000) is well below the Norfolk County market average for listed land, though a direct comparison is difficult because large conservation parcels are a distinct market.

The principal concerns are process-oriented rather than fundamental: the acreage discrepancy needs explanation, independent appraisal validation would strengthen the price case, the rollback tax waiver is a meaningful concession without public accounting, and the 16-month seller condition window raises questions about the readiness of the site for transfer. The environmental condition of the former landscape business site is an open question that the appropriation buffer partially acknowledges.

None of these concerns is necessarily disqualifying, but they warrant direct answers before Town Meeting authorization is given.
