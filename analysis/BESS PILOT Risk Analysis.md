# BESS PILOT Risk Analysis
## 2026 West Street Agreement — Regional Comparison and Underappreciated Risks

---

## Part 1: How Medway's Deal Compares to Recent New England BESS PILOTs

Comparable data for BESS PILOT agreements in Massachusetts is limited because utility-scale battery storage is still new to the region. ISO New England's first major standalone BESS facility only recently came online. However, two reference points are now available, and the state's 5,000 MW procurement mandate (signed into law November 2024) means many more will follow.

### Known Comparables

**Medway — 2026 West Street (Hecate Energy)**
300 MW AC / 4-hour (1,200 MWh implied). Annual payment: $3,047,556 PILOT + $91,427 CPA = $3,138,982. Per-MW annual rate: **$10,463/MW**. 20-year total: $62.8 million. Flat, no escalator.

**Carver — Cranberry Point (approved October 2025)**
150 MW BESS. Annual payment: over $1.6 million. Per-MW annual rate: approximately **$10,667/MW**. 20-year term. The Carver PILOT was described as representing about 4% of the town's annual revenue.

**Medway — Prior Milford Street Draft (Medway Grid/Eolian, 2024, never executed)**
250 MW AC / 500 MWh. Annual payment: $2,306,922. Per-MW annual rate: **$9,228/MW**. Never built.

**Tewksbury — Hillman Energy Center (proposed, East Point Energy)**
125 MW / 500 MWh. 20-year contract. PILOT terms have been discussed but specific dollar figures are not yet publicly confirmed in the sources reviewed.

### What the Comparables Suggest

On a per-MW basis, Medway's 2026 agreement ($10,463/MW/year) is within the range established by Carver's recently approved deal ($10,667/MW/year). Medway's rate is marginally lower — roughly $200/MW/year less — which over 300 MW and 20 years amounts to about $1.2 million in aggregate. Whether this gap is meaningful depends on how the two projects differ in other dimensions (site characteristics, transmission, storage duration, etc.).

The more important takeaway is that both deals share the same structural weakness: they are flat, fixed-payment contracts with no inflation adjustment over 20 years.

## Part 2: Construction Cost vs. PILOT Revenue — Is Medway Being Fairly Compensated?

### What the Project Likely Costs to Build

Industry sources (NREL, Ember, and market analysts) place utility-scale 4-hour LFP battery storage installation costs in the range of $800,000–$1,200,000 per MW as of 2025–2026, with competitive projects coming in lower. For a 300 MW / 4-hour system:

- **Conservative estimate:** $240 million (at $800K/MW)
- **Mid-range estimate:** $300 million (at $1M/MW)
- **High estimate:** $360 million (at $1.2M/MW)

These figures are for the battery system, balance-of-plant, and site work. They do not include the 2,755-foot 345kV overhead transmission line, substation, or land acquisition, which could add $30–80 million depending on complexity. A reasonable all-in capital cost estimate for the complete Medway project is **$270–440 million**.

### What Medway Would Collect Under Ad Valorem Taxation

Medway's FY2026 commercial/industrial tax rate would need to be applied to the assessed value. Assessed value for a new facility would typically be based on cost of construction less depreciation. In Year 1, before any depreciation, a $300 million assessed value at a hypothetical $18 per $1,000 tax rate would yield roughly **$5.4 million** — substantially more than the $3.14 million PILOT.

The PILOT agreement explicitly states it is intended to reflect "full and fair cash value." Yet the payment schedule implies an effective assessed value of approximately $170 million (at $18/$1,000), which is well below even conservative construction cost estimates. The "No Precedent" clause (Section 10) prevents the town from using this valuation as a benchmark for any other negotiation.

### The Depreciation Tradeoff

The argument for the PILOT's lower initial payment is that it does not decline over time. Under ad valorem taxation, the assessed value would fall as the equipment depreciates — potentially dramatically, since battery systems have a functional life of 10–20 years. The PILOT locks in a flat $3.14 million even as the tax basis would otherwise shrink toward zero.

This is a genuine benefit. But it is only a good deal if the flat rate is set at a level that fairly splits the difference between high early-year value and low late-year value. Whether $3.14 million achieves that balance depends on assumptions about depreciation schedules, replacement investment, and discount rates that neither the PILOT agreement nor public discussions appear to address transparently.

## Part 3: Risks the Town May Be Underweighting

### Risk 1: Inflation Erosion Is the Single Largest Financial Risk

This is not a new observation — it appears in the detractor arguments already written. But the magnitude deserves emphasis. At 3% annual inflation:

| Year | Nominal Payment | Real Value (2026 dollars) |
|------|----------------|--------------------------|
| 1    | $3,138,982     | $3,138,982               |
| 5    | $3,138,982     | $2,789,000               |
| 10   | $3,138,982     | $2,406,000               |
| 15   | $3,138,982     | $2,075,000               |
| 20   | $3,138,982     | $1,790,000               |

The cumulative real value of the 20-year stream at 3% inflation is approximately **$48.1 million** — not $62.8 million. At 4% inflation (not unreasonable given recent history), real cumulative value drops to approximately **$44.4 million**.

The Carver PILOT has the same structural problem. Both towns are accepting contracts where the developer's real cost of compliance declines every year while the town's real expenses increase. Union contracts, health insurance, and school budgets all rise with inflation. The PILOT does not.

**What a reasonable escalator would look like:** A 2% annual escalator would bring the 20-year nominal total to approximately $76.3 million. A CPI-linked adjustment would track actual inflation. Either mechanism is standard in long-term commercial leases and public-private agreements. The absence of any escalator is a choice that favors the developer.

### Risk 2: Battery Degradation and Replacement Are Not Addressed

Lithium-ion batteries degrade. Manufacturers typically guarantee no more than 2% annual capacity fade, meaning after 15 years the system may retain only 70% of its original capacity, and after 20 years roughly 65%. Many industry sources recommend replacement when capacity drops to 60% of the original rating.

This creates two problems the PILOT does not address:

**a) The developer will likely need to replace or augment battery modules during the 20-year term.** This represents a major capital reinvestment — potentially $50–150 million — that increases the project's total assessed value and economic life. Under ad valorem taxation, that reinvestment would be captured in reassessed property value. Under the flat PILOT, it is invisible to the town. The town receives $3.14 million whether the developer has invested $300 million or $450 million in the site.

**b) The capacity adjustment clause only triggers if the project expands beyond 305 MW.** Replacing degraded batteries with newer, higher-density modules that maintain or restore 300 MW output does not trigger a payment adjustment. The developer benefits from improved technology; the town does not.

### Risk 3: Decommissioning Liability Is Not in the PILOT

The PILOT agreement addresses payment terms but does not establish a decommissioning bond or surety. Massachusetts model bylaws for BESS facilities recommend requiring a surety bond at 125% of the estimated removal and site restoration cost, funded before construction begins.

Decommissioning a 300 MW battery facility on 11 developed acres is not trivial. Industry estimates for BESS decommissioning range widely, but a facility of this scale could cost $5–15 million to fully remove and remediate, depending on environmental conditions and salvage value of components.

If decommissioning requirements exist, they are presumably in the Host Community Agreement (HCA) dated January 1, 2025, or in the special permit conditions. But the PILOT's "independence" clause (Section 11) means that default under the HCA does not affect the PILOT, and vice versa. This raises a question: if the developer defaults on decommissioning obligations under the HCA but continues making PILOT payments, does the town have adequate leverage to compel site restoration?

### Risk 4: The Developer Is a Project-Specific LLC

Medway Energy Center, LLC is a single-purpose entity. Hecate Energy LLC (Chicago) is the parent, and it has a substantial track record — over 12 GW of projects developed and sold since 2012, with Hecate Grid (the storage-focused arm) backed by InfraRed Capital Partners. But the PILOT is between the town and the LLC, not the parent.

If the project becomes uneconomic, the LLC can be dissolved or allowed to default without exposing the parent company's other assets. The early termination provisions give the developer multiple exit paths (Force Majeure, regulatory action, permanent decommissioning). The town's remedy for default is to void the PILOT and revert to ad valorem taxation — but if the LLC is insolvent or the facility is abandoned, there may be no entity to tax and no assets worth taxing (batteries depreciate rapidly; the real estate value of an industrial battery site is uncertain).

This is not unique to Hecate or to this project — it is standard practice in project finance. But the PILOT does not include a parent company guarantee, a letter of credit, or any other credit enhancement that would protect the town if the LLC fails.

### Risk 5: The Missing DOR Deadline Creates Legal Ambiguity

The prior Milford Street draft required DOR approval within 30 days or the PILOT was void. The 2026 agreement requires DOR approval but sets no deadline. If DOR requests revisions, delays review, or imposes conditions, the agreement exists in a gray area — voted by Town Meeting, signed by the Select Board, but not legally effective.

During this period, the developer may proceed with permitting and construction activities that create community expectations and political pressure. If DOR ultimately requires material changes to the payment terms, the town may find itself in a weakened negotiating position because the project is already underway.

### Risk 6: No Revenue Sharing in Upside Scenarios

Battery storage revenue is driven by energy arbitrage, capacity payments, and ancillary services in ISO-NE markets. These revenue streams are volatile and have significant upside potential — particularly as fossil fuel retirements accelerate and the grid becomes more dependent on storage.

The PILOT captures none of this upside. If the project earns $80 million per year in market revenue, the town still receives $3.14 million. If market conditions improve dramatically (as many analysts expect as Massachusetts pursues 5,000 MW of storage by 2030), the gap between what the developer earns and what the town receives will widen.

Some municipal agreements in other energy sectors include modest revenue-sharing provisions (e.g., 1–2% of gross revenue above a threshold). The Medway PILOT includes no such mechanism.

### Risk 7: Forested Land Conversion Has Uncompensated Environmental Costs

Eleven acres of forest clearing and 47 acres of project footprint represent a loss of carbon sequestration, stormwater absorption, habitat, and aesthetic value. These are real costs borne by the community. The PILOT payment does not include any environmental mitigation component.

The irony is that a facility justified by clean energy goals achieves those goals partly by destroying a carbon sink. The net environmental calculation may still be positive — battery storage reduces fossil fuel use — but the local environmental costs are concentrated in Medway while the grid reliability benefits are diffused across the SEMA zone.

Some host community agreements include tree replacement ratios, conservation fund contributions, or impervious surface mitigation payments. It is unclear whether the HCA addresses these, but the PILOT does not.

### Risk 8: 345kV Overhead Transmission Line Risks

The 2,755-foot overhead 345kV transmission line is a permanent visual and physical change to the landscape. Unlike the prior Milford Street project (which used underground transmission), this line will be visible to neighbors and may affect property values along its route.

The PILOT does not include any payment or fund to compensate abutting property owners for potential diminution in value. The transmission line runs through Eversource-controlled parcels via easement, meaning the town has no direct control over its maintenance, appearance, or future modification.

## Part 4: What a Stronger Agreement Would Include

Based on the risks identified above, a stronger PILOT for the town would include some or all of the following provisions:

1. **An inflation escalator** — 2% annual increase or CPI-linked adjustment, applied to the base PILOT and CPA.

2. **A capital reinvestment clause** — if the developer invests more than a threshold amount (e.g., $20 million) in battery replacement or capacity augmentation, the PILOT payment is recalculated based on the updated investment.

3. **A decommissioning surety** — a bond or letter of credit at 125% of estimated removal cost, funded before COD, updated every 5 years.

4. **A parent company guarantee or credit enhancement** — Hecate Energy LLC (or a creditworthy affiliate) guarantees the LLC's PILOT obligations.

5. **A DOR approval deadline** — 60 or 90 days after Town Meeting vote, with automatic termination if not achieved.

6. **A revenue-sharing provision** — a modest percentage (1–2%) of gross revenue above a defined threshold, payable annually.

7. **An environmental mitigation fund** — a one-time or annual contribution to a conservation or tree replacement fund, sized to the acreage cleared.

8. **Property value protection** — a fund or abatement mechanism for abutting property owners who can demonstrate diminution in value attributable to the transmission line or facility.

None of these provisions would make the project uneconomic. Many are standard in comparable public-private agreements. Their absence in the Medway PILOT represents value left on the table.
