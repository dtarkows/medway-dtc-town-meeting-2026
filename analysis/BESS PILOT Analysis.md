# BESS PILOT Agreement Analysis
## 2026 West Street Agreement — Summary, Comparison, and Arguments

---

## Part 1: Summary of the 2026 PILOT Agreement

**Parties**
- **Town of Medway**, signed by Todd Alessandri, Chair, Select Board (April 6, 2026)
- **Medway Energy Center, LLC** (c/o Hecate Energy LLC, Chicago, IL) — developer signature not yet affixed, pending Town Meeting approval

**The Project**
A 300 MW AC, 4-hour battery energy storage system (BESS) to be constructed at and behind 15 West Street on approximately 47 acres (6 tax parcels). Roughly 11 acres will be actively developed for the battery enclosures and on-site substation. The land is currently forested. The project includes a new overhead 345kV transmission line approximately 2,755 feet long connecting to the existing West Medway Substation, running through Eversource-controlled parcels via easement.

**Purpose**
ISO-NE selected the project to enhance grid reliability in the Southeastern Massachusetts (SEMA) transmission zone and support the Commonwealth's clean energy goals for 2026–2031.

**Term**
20 years from the Commercial Operation Date (COD). No payments are owed during construction. Year 1 payment is due in full within 25 days of COD; subsequent payments are quarterly.

**Dollar Amounts**

| Year | PILOT | CPA (3%) | Total |
|------|-------|----------|-------|
| 1–20 | $3,047,556 | $91,427 | $3,138,982 |
| **20-year total** | **$60,951,120** | **$1,828,540** | **$62,779,660** |

Payments are **fixed for the full 20 years** — they will not increase with inflation, tax rate changes, or property revaluation, and will not decrease with depreciation.

**CPA Surcharge**
The CPA component (3% of the base PILOT) is paid annually by September 1st. It is separate from and in addition to the quarterly PILOT payments.

**Capacity Adjustment**
If the project expands beyond 300 MW by more than 5 MW, the payment schedule is revised pro rata. If the parties cannot agree within 30 days, the dispute goes to the three-step resolution process (informal negotiation → mediation → Norfolk County Superior Court).

**Early Termination (Developer's Rights)**
Medway Energy Center may terminate the PILOT before COD at any time, or after COD if: (i) the project is physically unable to operate for 3+ consecutive months due to casualty or Force Majeure; (ii) an adverse legal/regulatory order prevents construction or operation; or (iii) the project is permanently decommissioned.

**Early Termination (Town's Rights)**
After Year 18, the Town may notify the developer of intent to terminate the PILOT effective June 30, 2048 (end of Year 20 window), and negotiate a successor agreement. If no successor is reached, the project reverts to standard ad valorem taxation under G.L. c. 59.

**Default**
Late payments accrue interest under G.L. c. 59 §57. The Town issues a notice of default; the developer has 30 days to cure. Failure to cure gives the Town the option to void the PILOT and revert to ad valorem taxation.

**Conditions Precedent**
The agreement takes legal effect only upon: (i) Town Meeting approval; (ii) Massachusetts Department of Revenue (DOR) approval; and (iii) achievement of COD. If DOR objects, the PILOT is null and void.

**Relationship to HCA**
The PILOT is independent of the Host Community Agreement (HCA) dated January 1, 2025. Default under one does not constitute default under the other.

## Part 2: Comparison to the Prior BESS PILOT (Milford Street / Medway Grid)

The prior agreement was executed in 2024 between the Town and **Medway Grid, LLC** (c/o Eolian, L.P., Burlingame, CA) for a 250 MW BESS at 49, 53, and 55 Milford Street. Town Meeting authorized it as Article 16 of the 2024 ATM. That facility was constructed and is currently operating — giving the town direct experience with what a utility-scale BESS looks like in practice.

### Understanding MW vs. MWh

Battery facilities have two distinct capacity figures that are often cited together but measure different things:

- **MW (megawatts)** is *power* — how fast the facility can deliver electricity at any given moment, like the width of a pipe. A higher MW rating means the plant can serve more load simultaneously.
- **MWh (megawatt-hours)** is *energy* — the total amount of electricity stored in the batteries, like the volume of a tank. Higher MWh means the facility can run for longer before it needs to recharge.
- **Duration** is simply MWh ÷ MW. The new West Street facility is rated at 300 MW / 4 hours = **1,200 MWh**. The operating Milford Street facility is 250 MW / 500 MWh = **2-hour duration**.

A short-duration facility (2 hours) is useful for smoothing out demand spikes. A long-duration facility (4 hours) can also carry the grid through longer stress events — for example, a hot afternoon that stretches into the evening. ISO-NE selected West Street specifically because the SEMA region needs this longer-duration capability.

### Key Observations

**Revenue:** The 2026 agreement pays **$832,060 more per year** than the prior agreement — a 36% increase. Over 20 years, the additional revenue to the town is approximately **$16.6 million**. On a per-MW basis, the new agreement pays roughly 13.5% more per megawatt, even though it is a larger project (economies of scale often push per-unit rates down, so this is notable).

**Scale:** The new project is 20% larger in power output (300 vs. 250 MW), but **140% larger in energy storage capacity** (1,200 vs. 500 MWh). Because the number of physical battery modules scales with energy capacity, the West Street facility will contain roughly **2.4 times as many battery cabinets** as the operating Milford Street plant. It occupies more than four times the total acreage, though only about twice the actively developed area.

**Site character:** The Milford Street project would have demolished three residential homes, a barn, and an auto repair shop on 10.6 acres of already-developed land. The West Street project converts 11 acres of forested land on a 47-acre parcel. These represent different tradeoffs: one displaces residents and businesses; the other converts undeveloped green space.

**Transmission:** The prior project used underground transmission (~1,325 ft); the new project uses overhead lines (~2,755 ft). Overhead lines are generally less expensive to install but more visible.

**Payment structure:** The two agreements are structurally identical — flat fixed payments, no escalator, no depreciation adjustment, same Force Majeure provisions, same cure periods, same dispute resolution ladder. The 2026 agreement omits a specific 30-day deadline for DOR approval that appeared in the 2024 draft; the new agreement simply requires DOR approval without a time limit, which is a minor but meaningful difference that could delay the commencement of the PILOT term.

**Developer track record:** The prior Milford Street facility (Medway Grid/Eolian) was executed and is currently operating. This gives the town direct experience with what a utility-scale BESS looks like in practice, and establishes that the PILOT structure can successfully attract and deliver a built facility. It also means this is the town's second BESS PILOT, not a first experiment.

## Part 3: Arguments

### Arguments a Supporter Would Make

**1. Substantial, guaranteed revenue with no downside risk.**
The Town will receive $3.14 million every year for 20 years regardless of whether the facility depreciates, whether the tax rate changes, or whether the state legislature adjusts how energy storage is assessed. Unlike ad valorem property taxes on commercial or industrial facilities, which can shrink as equipment ages, this PILOT is a locked-in revenue stream. That's the equivalent of adding roughly 1% to the town's operating budget from a single source, with zero administrative overhead in collection.

**2. A significantly better deal than what came before.**
The Milford Street PILOT — approved by Town Meeting in 2024 — would have paid $2.3 million per year. This agreement pays $832,000 more annually for a comparable facility type. The town's negotiators extracted better per-MW terms even as the project grew larger. Town Meeting voters can see that the town did not simply accept the developer's first offer.

**3. The land use impact is lower than the prior project.**
Unlike the Milford Street project, this facility does not require demolishing occupied homes or displacing a business. The West Street site is forested, undeveloped land. Residents and businesses on Milford Street are not affected. The footprint, while larger in total acreage, is less disruptive to the existing community fabric.

**4. Grid reliability benefits the whole region.**
ISO-NE selected this facility specifically to shore up grid reliability in Southeastern Massachusetts. New England's grid faces increasing stress from the retirement of fossil fuel plants and the intermittent nature of solar and wind. A 300 MW / 4-hour storage facility can absorb excess generation and release it during peak demand, reducing the risk of blackouts. Medway residents benefit from this as ratepayers, not just as taxpayers.

**5. It supports state clean energy commitments the town has already endorsed.**
Massachusetts has set legally binding clean energy targets. Large-scale storage is not optional infrastructure — it is required for those goals to be achievable. Approving this PILOT puts Medway on the right side of that policy direction without requiring any town expenditure.

**6. The CPA fund gets a permanent boost.**
The $91,427 annual CPA contribution flows directly to the Community Preservation Fund, supporting open space, historic preservation, and housing initiatives. Over 20 years that is $1.83 million for community priorities that otherwise compete for limited funding.

### Arguments a Detractor Would Make

**1. The payment is fixed — which means the town loses as time passes.**
$3.14 million in 2030 is not the same as $3.14 million in 2045. At even a modest 3% inflation rate, the real value of Year 20 payments will be less than half of Year 1 in purchasing power. The town is locking in a fixed dollar amount on a 20-year contract with no cost-of-living adjustment while its own expenses will rise every year. Every other major contract the town signs — union agreements, vendor contracts — includes escalators. Why does this one not?

**2. The town may be significantly undervaluing the project.**
Both parties agree the PILOT is intended to reflect "full and fair cash value." A 300 MW battery storage facility with a 4-hour duration represents roughly $600–900 million in capital investment at current market rates for utility-scale storage. If the town's tax rate were applied to even a conservative assessed value, the tax obligation could exceed the PILOT payment significantly. The "No Precedent" clause (Section 10) means the town can never use these numbers to argue for higher payments elsewhere — a one-sided protection that benefits the developer.

**3. COD can be years away — and the town has no leverage during construction.**
The PILOT payments do not begin until the Commercial Operation Date (COD). Between Town Meeting approval and COD, construction and interconnection typically take two to four years. During that window, the land is committed, the community has organized around the project, and the town has no ability to renegotiate — but receives nothing. The developer retains the right to terminate before COD for any reason at any time. The Milford Street facility did eventually get built, but residents who live near the West Street site will experience years of construction activity before the first payment arrives.

**4. The developer can walk away; the town cannot.**
Once the PILOT is in effect, the town cannot unilaterally terminate it (except after Year 18 notice). The developer, however, can invoke Force Majeure, regulatory proceedings, or operational failure to exit the agreement. This asymmetry means the town bears the risk of lengthy construction delays, permitting failures, or changed business conditions at the developer level, while having no reciprocal ability to renegotiate if the project becomes unexpectedly profitable.

**5. 47 acres of forested land will be permanently converted.**
What is presented as a lower-impact site than Milford Street is still a significant land use change. Eleven acres of trees will be cleared and replaced with industrial electrical infrastructure. The remaining 36 acres of the parcel will be part of the project. This is a permanent change to the character of West Street, adjacent to an area that includes residential neighborhoods. The visual and environmental impact — clearing, construction traffic, overhead transmission lines, and industrial fencing — will be lasting and is not compensated in the PILOT payment.

**6. No DOR approval deadline creates an open-ended condition.**
The 2024 Milford Street draft required DOR to approve within 30 days or the PILOT was void. This agreement has no such deadline. The PILOT is conditioned on DOR approval but without a time limit. If DOR delays or requests revisions, the parties are in limbo — the Town Meeting has voted approval, the town is in a de facto relationship with the developer, but the agreement has no legal force. This ambiguity could complicate future decisions about the site.

**7. Hecate Energy is an out-of-state company with no community ties.**
The developer is a Delaware LLC with offices in Chicago. There are no provisions for local hiring, local contracting preferences, or community benefit funds beyond the PILOT itself. Once built, a facility like this employs a handful of maintenance technicians. The town receives its payment, but the economic relationship is otherwise thin. Compare this to a local business that generates property taxes, employs residents, and circulates money in the local economy.
